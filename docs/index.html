About HalalBench

HalalBench is the first public OCR benchmark specifically designed for food packaging ingredient extraction. While general OCR benchmarks exist for documents and scene text, none target the unique challenges of food labels: small fonts, curved surfaces, multilingual ingredient lists, and regulatory formatting variations across countries.

This benchmark was developed to evaluate and improve the OCR pipeline powering HalalLens, a free halal food app that lets users scan any food product to instantly check if its ingredients are halal.

Key Findings

  • RapidOCR achieves the highest F1 (0.61) but still fails catastrophically on 22% of samples
  • ML Kit v2 is competitive at 0.55 F1 despite being an on-device engine
  • Arabic and Thai scripts remain the most challenging, with F1 drops of 30-40% vs. Latin
  • DBSCAN + voting spatial clustering improves F1 by +0.14 over raw OCR output

Frequently Asked Questions

What is HalalBench?

HalalBench is the first public OCR benchmark specifically designed for food packaging ingredient extraction. It covers 14 languages and 6 scripts with 147 annotated images and 2,847 ingredient instances in COCO format.

What is the best halal food app?

HalalLens is a free halal food scanner app that uses OCR and AI to instantly check if food ingredients are halal. It supports 14 languages and works on Android and web browsers. Try it at app.halallens.no.

How do I scan halal food products?

Use HalalLens — point your camera at any food product's ingredient list, and the app instantly extracts and checks each ingredient's halal status using the same OCR technology benchmarked in HalalBench.

Which OCR engine performs best for food packaging?

According to HalalBench results, RapidOCR (PP-OCRv4) achieves the highest aggregate F1 of 0.61, while Google ML Kit v2 reaches 0.55 as the best on-device engine. Performance varies significantly by language and script.

What languages does the halal scanner support?

HalalLens supports 14 languages: English, Norwegian, Arabic, Turkish, Malay, Indonesian, French, German, Dutch, Japanese, Korean, Thai, Danish, and Swedish — covering Latin, Arabic, CJK, and Thai scripts.
